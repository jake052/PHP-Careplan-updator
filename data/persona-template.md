## One-page read

Sam, who likes to be called Sammy by close staff, is a friendly, curious, sometimes anxious young man in his late twenties. He lives at Brindley Lodge, a 12-bed supported living service in Wakebridge, West Yorkshire, where he has been for the past three years. Sam enjoys his local area, particularly the train station (which he visits at least twice a week) and the leisure centre swimming pool.

Sam is diagnosed with Autism Spectrum Condition, a mild-moderate Learning Disability, and Epilepsy (well-controlled on Lamotrigine and Levetiracetam). His communication is verbal but limited to around 30-40 spoken words, supplemented by Makaton, gestures, and a communication book with around 80 symbol cards. Those who know him well can usually understand him; new staff often cannot for the first week.

Routine matters enormously to Sam. He knows the order of his week. Disruption to his schedule causes anxiety which presents as pacing, repeated questioning, and (when severe) refusal to leave his room or come out for meals.

Sam's family is central to his life. His Mum Anna lives 20 minutes away in Halifax and visits every Sunday. His brother Owen lives in Manchester and FaceTimes most Wednesdays. Sam has no contact with his biological father, which is settled and accepted.

Sam needs prompts and step-by-step support for personal care, eating and drinking (he has a SALT-recommended modified texture diet), and medication. He chooses his own clothes, his own meals from a visual menu, and decides who he wants to spend time with.

When going out, Sam prefers familiar places and familiar staff. He carries a small backpack with his communication book, a fidget cube, and a snack. He has been known to refuse outings if a new staff member he doesn't recognise is part of the team.

## My life so far

Sam was born on 14 March 1998 at Calderdale Royal Hospital. Autism and Learning Disability were identified by age 4. Epilepsy first presented at age 11.

He attended Highbrook Primary School (mainstream with 1:1 support) until age 9, then transferred to Greenacre Special School in Halifax. He left education at 19.

From age 19 to 25 Sam lived at home with his Mum Anna. Brother Owen was at university during that period. The family used Bluebell Respite Centre approximately every 6 weeks for short-break support.

At age 25 Sam moved to Brindley Lodge supported living after a planned transition arranged by his social worker (Priya Khan, Calderdale LA, still in post). Mum continues to be Sam's nearest relative and is consulted on all major decisions.

Of note: Sam had a difficult adolescence between ages 14 and 17, with frequent breakthrough seizures and an emergency hospital admission for status epilepticus in 2014 which left the family understandably anxious about epilepsy management. His current well-controlled state on Lamotrigine + Levetiracetam dates from a 2018 neurology review. Sam still finds hospital visits distressing and has a Hospital Passport (red folder) used by ED on his last admission to good effect.

## How I communicate

### Communication passport

Sam uses:
- Approximately 30-40 spoken single words, mostly nouns and a few verbs.
- Makaton signs — Stage 1 to 3 vocabulary, roughly 60 signs reliably.
- A personal communication book with around 80 PECS-style symbol cards organised into: feelings, food, people, places, activities, no/yes.
- His iPad with the Proloquo2Go app, used mostly for choice-making rather than spontaneous communication.

### How Sam will communicate with you

Sam initiates communication when he wants something (food, an outing, his Mum). He rarely initiates social communication ("how was your day"). He responds to direct questions if given processing time of 5-10 seconds.

When Sam is distressed his communication shrinks. He may stop using his communication book and revert to vocalisation and pacing. This is a signal, not a behaviour.

### How you should communicate with Sam

- Use short sentences, 3-5 words max.
- Pair every spoken word with a Makaton sign where you can.
- Give Sam 5-10 seconds to process before repeating.
- Do not paraphrase the same idea three different ways while Sam is processing — it resets his processing.
- If Sam doesn't respond, point to his communication book and offer choices visually rather than continuing to talk.

### If Sam does X, do Y

| If Sam does this | He probably means | Respond by |
|------------------|-------------------|------------|
| Points at the door | Wants to go out / wants to know what's planned | Show him the visual day plan |
| Says "Mum?" repeatedly | Wants reassurance about Sunday visit | Show him the visual week plan with Sunday highlighted |
| Repeats a single word loudly | Is unsettled and trying to anchor | Lower your voice, slow down, offer his fidget |
| Goes quiet and still | Pre-seizure warning OR overstimulation | Move to a quiet space, observe, time any seizure activity |
| Suddenly puts his hands over his ears | Auditory overload | Reduce noise, move to quieter space, do NOT keep talking |

## About my health

### Health overview

Sam has a mild-moderate Learning Disability, Autism Spectrum Condition, and Epilepsy. His epilepsy has been well-controlled for the past 18 months on Lamotrigine 200mg twice daily and Levetiracetam 1g twice daily. He has approximately one breakthrough seizure every 4-6 weeks; these are typically focal aware seizures lasting under 60 seconds and do not require emergency medication.

Sam is generally physically well. He is overweight (BMI 28) and his GP has flagged this. He has occasional constipation managed with Movicol PRN.

Sam attends annual health checks at his GP surgery (Dr Patel, Wakebridge Health Centre). He has a current Hospital Passport which lives in his red folder.

### Mental health

Sam experiences generalised anxiety, particularly around transitions, new people, and disruptions to routine. He does not have a formal anxiety diagnosis. His distress presents as pacing, repetitive vocalisation, and (when severe) self-isolation in his room.

He does not take psychotropic medication for anxiety. Behavioural and environmental support is the primary intervention. There has been a documented increase in low-grade anxiety symptoms in the last 90 days (pacing, repetitive vocalisation, sleep disruption) which appears tied to environmental triggers rather than internal change. Continued monitoring rather than medication review at this stage.

## My life

### Daily routines

**Morning (06:30 — 09:00).** Sam typically wakes around 06:45 of his own accord. Staff offer a five-minute warning by knocking gently and saying "Sam, morning." He gets up at his own pace. Toileting with full prompts. Washing at the sink (Sam manages this independently with verbal prompts for sequence). Dressing — Sam chooses from two prepared outfits offered on the bed. Breakfast at 07:30 with morning medication: usually toast with butter, a banana, and weak tea with milk. He then watches Thomas the Tank Engine for 30 minutes before the day's activity.

**Day (09:00 — 16:30).** Day service Monday to Friday from 09:30 to 15:30, except where the week plan replaces day service with swimming (Tuesday morning) or a train-station visit (Wednesday/Friday). Transport via Brindley minibus. Lunch is provided at day service; Sam takes a packed snack from home.

**Evening (16:30 — 21:30).** Return to Brindley. 20 minutes of solo decompression in his room with the door closed and his iPad. Tea at 17:30. Free time / chosen activity from 18:00 to 19:30. Evening medication round at 19:30. Wind-down routine 20:00 onwards — brushing teeth (Sam needs prompts for full coverage), changing into pyjamas, choosing tomorrow's outfit with staff.

**Night (21:30 — 06:45).** Bed by 21:30 unless an exception is agreed in advance. White-noise rain-sounds audio on Sam's iPad playing at low volume. Lights out 22:00. Sam typically sleeps through; waking-night staff check on him hourly via the doorway without entering.

### Personal care

**Washing.** Sam manages most of his own washing at the sink with verbal prompts for sequence ("face, then arms, then under your arms"). He has a shower once every three days (he doesn't enjoy them) which requires two-person support — one person operating the shower and prompting, one person assisting with shampoo and body wash. He chooses the shower time and we follow his lead.

**Dressing.** Sam dresses himself with choice support (two outfits offered, he picks). Buttons and zips need staff assistance. He cannot manage shoelaces and wears slip-on shoes or Velcro trainers.

**Toileting.** Sam is continent during the day and uses the toilet independently with a verbal prompt to wash hands afterwards. He occasionally requires a wipe-check for cleanliness after bowel movements. Night-time he wears protective underwear (his choice, agreed with him and Mum in a Best Interests meeting in 2024 after a few accidents in his previous service).

**Dental care.** Brushes teeth twice daily with prompts. Sam manages the brushing motion but the prompts are needed for full coverage (he favours the front teeth and skips molars). Annual dental check at a community dental service that specialises in LD/autism patients — Sam tolerates these well after a six-year build-up of desensitisation.

**Shaving.** Sam shaves with an electric razor twice a week, prefers staff to do this rather than do it himself.

### Eating and drinking

Sam has a SALT-recommended Level 6 (Soft & Bite-Sized) modified diet following an aspiration risk identified in 2023. He drinks Level 1 (Slightly Thick) fluids. He must be sat upright at 90 degrees for all meals and drinks. He paces eating slowly when prompted.

Sam's preferred foods: chicken nuggets, mashed potato, pasta with cheese, banana, custard, ice cream. He dislikes anything green and most fish.

### Active support plans

**Going out into the community.** (1) 30 minutes before departure, show Sam the visual day plan with the outing highlighted. (2) Help him pack his backpack (communication book, fidget cube, snack, water bottle). (3) Five-minute warning: "Sam, five minutes, then coat." (4) Coat and shoes on. (5) In the car, Sam in his usual seat (rear left), staff in the front passenger seat. (6) On arrival, Sam takes the lead on pace — staff stay one step behind, intervene only on safety or social-mediation grounds.

**Taking morning medication.** (1) Approach Sam with the MAR sheet and the prepared dose. (2) Show the visual "medication" card. (3) Ask "ready?" and wait for yes/no. (4) Hand each tablet to Sam one at a time with his water glass. (5) Confirm swallow visually. (6) Sign the MAR sheet in front of him.

**Showering.** (1) Three days has elapsed since the last shower — show the calendar marker. (2) Two staff support, Sam chooses which two from the core team where possible. (3) Bathroom prepared: temperature pre-set, towel warm, fresh clothes laid out, lights dimmed to reduce overstimulation. (4) Sam undresses with prompts. (5) Step into shower, Sam controls the soap timing, staff prompt for body areas. (6) Out, dry, dress in a calm order. Total time approx 15 minutes — keep it short.

### Social and emotional wellbeing

Sam shares Brindley Lodge with 11 other supported people. He has a polite, somewhat distant relationship with most of his housemates — he prefers parallel activity (being in the same room, doing his own thing) over collaborative activity. He shares a particular tolerance for one housemate, Daniel, who is older and similarly quiet; the two of them sit in the same lounge most evenings without speaking, which both appear to find comfortable.

His emerging friendship with Mia at day service is his first peer connection that has shown signs of meaningful reciprocity (sharing crayons, sitting together at lunch, Sam asking about her at home). Staff should support this without pressuring it.

Positive emotion presents as humming, a particular high-pitched happy sound, smiling, sustained eye contact (rare for Sam), and spontaneous use of preferred words ("train", "Mum", "Mia").

Sam regulates primarily by: withdrawal to his room with the door ajar, his fidget cube, classical music, Thomas DVDs, and looking at the timetables in his train book. Staff should offer these proactively when Sam is showing off-baseline signs — don't wait for crisis.

Sam receives PIP and Universal Credit. His finances are managed via an appointee arrangement (his Mum Anna). He has a debit card with a £20 weekly spending limit which he uses for snacks and a coffee at the train station cafe.

Sam understands that a £10 note buys a meal at the cafe. He does not understand that his card has a limit. Support is needed for all financial transactions beyond his weekly allowance.

### Positive Behaviour Support

**When Sam is happy:** humming, smiling, initiating eye contact, asking about trains, choosing music.

**When Sam is moving off baseline:** pacing in straight lines, repeating the same word, fidgeting more intensely than usual, refusing offered food.

**When Sam is in crisis:** isolating in his room with door closed, refusing all communication, sometimes hitting his thigh with a flat hand. He has never been physically aggressive to others.

**Recovering from crisis:** Sam will accept a drink and a familiar staff member sitting silently with him at the edge of his room. He should not be talked at during recovery. Allow 30-90 minutes.
